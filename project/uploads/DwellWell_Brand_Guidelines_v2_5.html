SECTION 01

Brand Foundation

Who DwellWell is, who it serves, and the single sentence every communication derives from.

Positioning

DwellWell is a US-based managed property maintenance and preservation operator — the operator of record between the demand side (property managers, landlords, lenders, real-estate investors) and vetted, independent US-based contractors. DwellWell receives work orders, manages the full job lifecycle from intake to closeout, dispatches contractors, and owns the client relationship throughout. When something goes wrong, the client deals with DwellWell — not the contractor.

Locked — what DwellWell is notDwellWell is not a gig platform, a marketplace, a directory, or a startup. It never adopts consumer-app, gig-economy, or home-services-franchise cues. "Platform" is prohibited in copy (§02).

The three brand words

Every output is tested against three words. If a sentence hedges it isn't Trustworthy; if it rambles it isn't Organised; if it wastes the reader's time it isn't Efficient.

WordIn practice
TrustworthyClaims are concrete and verifiable. Commitments are stated as facts, never aspirations.
OrganisedProcess is visible. Structure carries meaning — in layout and in language.
EfficientNothing redundant. The brand says what is needed and stops.

Personality

DwellWell speaks like a senior partner at a well-regarded professional firm — formal enough to command respect, human enough to treat clients as people. Calm, authoritative, competent. The test: "DwellWell is the most competent person in the room — and doesn't need to say so." If copy sounds like it is trying to impress, it is wrong; if it sounds like it is describing how things work, it is right.

SECTION 02

Verbal Identity

The governing voice and tone. Any writer, operator, or agency applying this section should be able to produce DwellWell-correct copy without further instruction.

Voice character

The brand trusts its audience. Property managers are skilled professionals who scan rather than read; they have been burned by vague promises before, and they do not need enthusiasm — they need evidence. DwellWell gives them evidence in the form of precise, process-led language that describes what happens, in what order, with what outcome.

Three-word test (Brand Brief v2.4): before publishing, ask whether the copy feels Trustworthy, Organised, and Efficient. If a sentence hedges (not trustworthy), rambles (not organised), or wastes the reader's time (not efficient) — rewrite it.

Two modes — one voice · Voice-person rule

The voice is constant; the mode shifts with surface. On the website and marketing it is process-led — "DwellWell" is the subject, with no experience claims. In proposals and credentials it is experience-anchored — the founding team's experience is the credibility signal, always attributed to the team, never the entity. The voice-person follows: "DwellWell" is the subject in brand/marketing copy; "we" is the subject in correspondence (emails, proposals, work orders, and website FAQ/contact). The shift is structural, not tonal — do not mix the two within a single module or section. A multi-part document may carry both: a proposal, for example, uses "DwellWell" in its cover and about sections and "we" through the body. When a surface is both marketing and correspondence (e.g. outbound sales email), correspondence wins — write in the first person, signed by a named person.

Five tone principles

Principle 01
Specific over general

Every claim is concrete and verifiable. Process language describes what happens, in what order, with what outcome. Abstractions — "quality service," "reliable results," "we care" — are not used: they cannot be tested and are not believed.

Do
"Submit a work order. DwellWell dispatches a vetted contractor, manages the job, and closes it with full documentation."
Don't
"We provide high-quality property maintenance solutions tailored to your needs."
Principle 02
Direct without being blunt

DwellWell says what needs saying and stops — no filler, no preamble, no unnecessary qualification. But directness is not curtness; the brand always respects the reader's intelligence and professional context.

Do
"One contact. Every job managed, documented, and closed by us — not referred out."
Don't
"We just wanted to quickly touch base and let you know that we might be able to assist with some of your maintenance needs."
Principle 03
Confident without overclaiming

Commitments are stated as operational facts, not aspirations. Where a claim is not yet established it is held back rather than hedged. A scoped claim stated firmly beats a broad claim stated softly. Never "we try" or "we aim to."

Do
"Emergency and urgent requests are acknowledged the same business day."
Don't
"We always try our best to respond to all requests as quickly as possible."
Principle 04
Process-led

Describing what happens — in sequence — is more persuasive to this audience than any adjective. The verbs matter: DwellWell "coordinates," "manages," "closes," and "documents" — never "connects," "matches," or "finds."

Do
"Work order in. Contractor dispatched. Job completed. Documentation delivered."
Don't
"Our seamless platform connects you with the best contractors instantly."
Principle 05
Peer-to-peer register

DwellWell speaks to property managers as the competent professionals they are — using their vocabulary, assuming their knowledge. "Work orders," "portfolio," "multifamily," "HOA," and "preservation" are used without definition. The brand never adopts a teaching register with its primary audience.

Do
"From routine work orders to emergency calls, DwellWell coordinates across your portfolio."
Don't
"A 'work order' is a request for maintenance. When you submit one to us, we get started right away!"

Messaging architecture

No copy may assert a claim not found here. Binding launch constraints: handyman + full preservation only; same-business-day acknowledgement for emergency/urgent only; coverage case-by-case; no warranty terms in copy; network size and contractor payment timing not claimed.

ThemeApproved claim & guardrail

Before & after — the working reference

Six pairs across all three audiences and five touchpoint types. The "before" is annotated with what fails; the "after" with what it does correctly.

01 · Website hero copy — PM audience

✗ Before

"Connect with trusted property maintenance professionals in your area. Fast, reliable service for all your needs. Get matched today."

"Connect" and "get matched" are marketplace signals; "in your area" implies a directory; three generic adjectives in a row. Nothing distinguishes it from Angi.

✓ After

"DwellWell coordinates property maintenance from intake to closeout. One contact. Vetted contractors. Every job documented."

"Coordinates" signals the operator in one word; the three-part sentence mirrors the anchor and scans; three specific facts, no adjectives.

02 · Service page, how it works — PM audience

✗ Before

"Our platform gives property managers access to skilled handymen. Post your job, get matched, track progress in the app."

"Platform," "post your job," "get matched," "track in the app" — marketplace and consumer-tech throughout. The client does all the work.

✓ After

"Submit a work order. DwellWell dispatches a vetted contractor, manages the job, and closes it out with full documentation. You deal with us — not the contractor."

A three-step sequence builds trust through specificity; "dispatches" and "closes it out" are the operator verbs; the final line makes accountability explicit.

03 · Outbound prospecting email, opener — PM audience

✗ Before

"Hi [Name]! Hope you're doing well. I wanted to introduce DwellWell — we're passionate about transforming property maintenance for busy property managers like you. Would love to connect!"

Three exclamation marks; "passionate about transforming" combines two banned phrases; condescending; says nothing about what DwellWell does.

✓ After

"Hi [Name], DwellWell manages property maintenance for portfolio managers — one contact, vetted contractors, every job coordinated and documented. If you're working with multiple vendors across your portfolio, it may be worth a conversation."

Opens with what DwellWell is; the pain point is implied, not accused; low-pressure close suits a sceptical first contact.

04 · Proposal, about section — PM audience (experience permitted)

✗ Before

"We are a new and exciting company committed to delivering the highest quality property maintenance services with a focus on exceptional customer satisfaction and outstanding results."

"New and exciting" is a self-inflicted credibility wound; three superlatives with no backing; could be any company in any industry.

✓ After

"The team behind DwellWell brings 12+ years of direct experience in US property maintenance and preservation operations. DwellWell is built on that experience — a managed operator model designed to replace the fragmentation of multiple contractors with a single, accountable point of coordination."

Experience attributed to the team; "12+ years" is specific; the second sentence turns the claim into a description of the model.

05 · Preservation service page — preservation client

✗ Before

"We offer comprehensive property preservation services for banks, lenders, and investors. Our nationwide network handles everything from securing to winterisation. Fast turnaround. Competitive pricing."

"Comprehensive" and "everything" need a fully operational scope; "nationwide network" is unsupportable; "competitive pricing" undermines credibility with compliance-focused clients.

✓ After

"DwellWell coordinates property preservation services — securing, winterisation, debris removal, condition reporting, and vacant-property maintenance — with the documentation lenders and asset managers require. Every job is closed with a complete record."

A specific list replaces "everything"; speaks to the client's primary concern — compliance and audit readiness — without overstating the record.

06 · Contractor application page — contractor audience

✗ Before

"Love doing handiwork? Join thousands of pros on DwellWell and earn money doing what you do best! Flexible hours, great pay, work whenever you want."

Condescending to a professional; "thousands of pros" overclaims network size; "work whenever you want" is gig-economy framing — the opposite of an organised operator.

✓ After

"DwellWell provides work to vetted contractors across residential maintenance and property preservation. Clear scope on every job. Consistent dispatch. Apply to join the network."

"Clear scope" and "consistent dispatch" address what contractors value most; "apply to join" signals selectivity as a quality signal; same professional register as client copy.

Misuse — do not reproduce

  • Do not change colours or place the logo on an unapproved (off-palette) background.
  • Do not distort, stretch, or scale non-uniformly. Do not rotate — the logo is always upright.
  • Do not add shadows, glows, gradients, bevel, or emboss effects.
  • Do not outline the icon — it is a filled mark.
  • Do not rearrange components — icon, rule, and wordmark positions are fixed.
  • Do not place the wordmark on contrast below 4.5:1.
  • Do not append a tagline lockup — there is no fixed tagline (below).
